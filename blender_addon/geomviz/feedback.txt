"What is balance? It's a property of graphs with binary-coloured edges, which we can think of relationships between people."
"Two people can be friends (blue), enemies (red) or have no relationship (no edge)."
"Balance is a global property of the graph that, means that all the relationships are _consistent_, in some sense."

"If you look at all the possible triangles that can occur in this kind of graphs, you get four cases: everyone can be friends, one pair of friends can share an enemy, et cetera."
"A graph is called _strongly balanced_ if it only contains these triangles. _Weakly balanced_ graphs can also include 'hateful triangles'..."

"Why do we have these definitions? Because it turns out that strongly balanced graphs can always be partitioned into two enemy-free 'tribes',
and weakly balanced graphs partition into k tribes for k > 2. This is called George's Sad Childhood Theorem."
"In other words, balanced graphs have relationships that can be consistently explained by putting everyone in different tribes."

Slide Six:


PROBLEMS WITH REAL DATA

"The problem with the notion of balance as a global property of a graph is that it can be highly sensitive to individuals."
"For example, if I have a real social network that is _almost_ balanced, but there's one guy who hates everyone, then balance is destroyed."
"What we would like is a robust way of determining whether a real graph is balanced in an approximate sence."
